The Box in the Library

Daniel Hatch

I stumble upon this box by accident. Struggling to find pertinent research to study at the Frick in New York, I had leaped into this adventure before I looked, hoping I would find something, and with the plane ticket bought before I knew if the Frick Library was even open at that time, I perused their online catalog. I had discovered earlier that the Cooper-Hewitt had been closed for repairs, so I thought I need to prepare better for my research trip to New York.

I found the Frick online catalog difficult to search through and that there were very few images to evaluate the catalog entries. I had come across some books on Eric Gill, but I felt I seen too much of his work, and I needed to branch out. So after searching for Morrison, and Eric Gill, I stumbled across Josef Albers. I knew him only as they guy who wrote “The Interaction of Color”. I had purchased a black and white version for undergraduate school to save money as a starving student, but I had never really thought much about him. Intrigued that the catalog had indicated that a box of his writings, woodcuts, prints, and other documents resided at the Frick Reference Library.

When I arrived at the Frick, not sure what I would do with my daughter Emilee, who had accompanied me, I asked if she could stay with me. The library had a strict policy of only letting adults into the library, but fortunately a charming young lady acquiesced to my request to have my research assitant come into the library with me. So we entered an ancient elevator and ascended to the 3rd floor to the reading room.

I didn’t initially understand where this box came from with the Josef Albers papers, but I received an email from Shannon Morelli, the archivist who I worked with there, which entailed the origin of the box. On August 26, 1946, Josef Albers had written several museums to ask if they were interested in prints and other documents related to his teaching and his wife’s work (strangely I received these documents on August 26, 2013). He wrote from the Black Mountain College in North Carolina. Again in 1954 when Albers had moved to Yale, he had more materials to update the collection at the Frick. Again in 1958, 1960 and 1961 he sent more materials. It seems unlikely that Josef Albers had been self obsessed. So he must have had a genuine desire to enlarge the art community with his teaching efforts.

Transcripts of some of his talks seem to give the indication that he was not full of himself. “He who is and remains a student himself is justified to teach students.... How can we develop others when our own growth is arrested? If growth is the aim and the measure of development on the teachers’ as well as the students’ side, it is also its excitement without which teaching is only a hard job and a sour bread.” - Josef Albers

Later in August, I received some of the scans I had requested from the Frick. It had come view project from another department other than Shannon’s archives where I had found the box of Josef Albers papers. It was one of several books I had found of his in the library section. Two of these books caught my attention. Josef Albers Art as Experience and Josef Albers To Open Eyes. I think that maybe what I have been coming to terms with is how I expect to teach Graphic Design.

Maybe the bigger question is why should I teach? Is it purely to fill up my time? Am I yearning to make a difference in this industry I have devoted some 25 years of my life
