Alan Meeks, Typographic Artist

Daniel Hatch

I became interested in Alan Meeks’ typographic work as I researched the letter “Q” in roman typefaces. As I annotated the typefaces I was studying, I had discovered he had designed many of the familiar fonts that I was familiar with from my college days (e.g. VAG round, Brickley Script, Jazz, Plaza, Algerian, Cabaret, and Belwe). My initial research yielded very little biographical information about Alan. This utter lack of content on him intrigued me, with most of the information I found a reworked of Alan Meeks’ website verbiage. Several books seemed to mention people around him, but he was seemingly left out of typographic history. After a fairly library exhaustive library search, I decided I had to contact the designer, with hopes that he could direct me to a book, or biography about him. With that first email to the contact me address on his website, it started our correspondence over email.

Alan Meeks was born in 1951, to a stay at home ‘mum’ and an accountant father at the News of the World. He grew up with one brother, a fairly famous sports photographer in the UK. It was an auspicious start to Alan’s life. Neither of his parents were artistic, but that didn’t seem to inhibit Alan from being creative.

Captivated from the age of three by Walt Disney cartoons, Alan held up Walt Disney as an “overwhelming influence and inspiration”. He would draw Disney characters as a kid, although he reflected back that the drawings were not very good. What he came to admired about Walt Disney was the uncompromising quality of everything he produced. “no theme park will ever rival Disneyworld, Walt let nothing go out until he was completely satisfied with it regardless of the cost. Perfection was far more important than profit, and I felt everything was produced for the enjoyment of the customer and not the financial gain for the producer.”

He went to Lister technical college and in 1969 at the age of 18 started working for Graphic Systems, a rival to Letraset for transfer products. There he learned to cut rubylith film from Gary Gillot, their “best instructor in stencil cutting”. His technique copied the Japanese style of brush lettering. He would move the rubylith film with one hand, while cutting film with a stencil knife in the other hand. Both the paper and the knife were moving at the same time. “Gary taught me to consider the method of stencil cutting and its results as artwork and not simply a technical process.”

Graphic Systems could be classified as a frugal company. They required the stencil cutters to use every available scrap of Ulano (rubylith), so they would use the insides of large capital letters to cut smaller letters. With high pressure to complete work quickly, Alan had come into work after an injury with both hands in bandages, and his co-workers taped a stencil knife to his hand, so he could work.

At the age of 23 he left and started at the Letraset type studio (known originally for their rub-on lettering for paste-up in advertising). Letraset had a much more generous time allowance for artwork, a reprieve from his previous working conditions. He worked in the design studio which consisted of 2 people in London with the manufacturing facility in Ashford, Kent (100 miles outside of London). The design staff in London grew to 6 or 7 people. Alan had a good relationship with his former co-workers and regularly gets together with them.
